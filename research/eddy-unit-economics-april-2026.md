# Eddy Course Unit Economics: Can a $99-$199 Course Work With Paid Acquisition?
*Research memo — April 8, 2026*
*Question: For a $99-$199 async course teaching fundraising/pitching skills to early-stage founders, acquired via paid Meta ads through a quiz-based lead magnet funnel: (1) What are realistic lead-to-purchase conversion rates? (2) Does direct-to-course beat the lead magnet path? (3) At what price/structure does the math start working?*

## TL;DR

The math does not work for a $99-$199 standalone course sold via paid Meta cold traffic. At your best CPL ($39 from C1), you'd need 26% of leads to purchase at $149 just to break even — industry benchmarks for cold-traffic info products are 1-5%. This isn't fixable with funnel optimization; it's structural to the price point. There IS a demand pulse (43 quiz completions, 15 email captures, healthy CTRs), but the viable paths are: (a) price up to $497+, (b) use a self-liquidating offer funnel with a $27-$47 front end, (c) treat paid ads as list-building for a multi-product business (coaching + community + course), or (d) sell Eddy organically only. Option (c) is likely the right frame given HC/TNB's broader business model.

### Direct-to-Course (Skip Lead Magnet) — Also Doesn't Work

Cold-traffic course conversion from a sales page: **0.5-2%** of clicks (multiple sources). At $0.50-$2 CPC (Meta benchmarks for B2B from WordStream 2025) and 1% conversion rate:

- CPA = $50-$200 per customer
- At $149 price: break-even only if CPC stays under $1.50 AND conversion rate hits 1%+
- Meta's 2025 B2B CPC averages are well above $1.50

Verdict: marginally better than the lead magnet path, but still underwater and you lose the email list asset.

### Self-Liquidating Offer Model — The Only Paid Structure That Works

The SLO model is how sub-$200 courses actually monetize with paid traffic:

1. **Front-end offer:** $7-$47 product (mini-course, workshop recording, detailed assessment report)
2. **Order bump at checkout:** +$17-$37 add-on
3. **One-click upsell:** The full $149-$199 course

Case study (Monica Badiu, March 2026): $27 front-end course, 35% checkout conversion, ROAS of 2.5x, $29K revenue in 41 days. Key: order bumps and upsells pushed average order value to $51, covering the $27 ad cost and generating profit.

The front end breaks even on ad spend. The upsell is profit. You build a list of **buyers** (not just leads), who convert at 3-5x higher rates on back-end offers.

Source: Monica Badiu case study; Social Media Examiner SLO guide; Major Impact Media acquisition funnel framework

### Quiz Funnel Benchmarks (Your Funnel Specifically)

Your quiz is performing normally. The issue isn't quiz conversion — it's what happens after.

## Why It Works (Foundational Principles)

**Price elasticity and acquisition math:** For any direct-response channel, CPA must be less than the product's contribution margin. With digital products (near-zero COGS), that means CPA < price. When CPL alone exceeds the product price (as it does here at $39-72 CPL vs $99-$199 price), the required lead-to-purchase conversion rate becomes mathematically implausible.

**The LTV:CAC ratio (standard benchmark: 3:1 minimum):** At $149 course price and $72 blended CPL with 3% conversion rate, CAC = $2,400. LTV:CAC = 0.06:1. Even at C1's $39 CPL with 5% conversion: CAC = $780, LTV:CAC = 0.19:1. You need LTV of ~$2,340 per customer to hit 3:1 at blended CPL — which is only possible if the course feeds higher-ticket offers.

**Self-liquidating funnel logic (Russell Brunson / ClickFunnels framework):** The insight is that you don't need the front-end product to be profitable — you need it to cover ad costs. Profit comes from the back end. This only works when the front-end price is low enough that conversion rate × price ≥ CPC. At $27-$47 with 2-3% conversion and $1-$2 CPC, the math closes.

## Recommendation

### The Demand Pulse Is Real — But the Monetization Path Isn't Paid Ads → $149 Course

Evidence of demand:
- 43 quiz completions in 7 days from cold Meta traffic
- 15 email captures (35% quiz-to-lead)
- CTR of 4-7% on founder-targeted creative (well above Meta's 1.5-2.5% average)
- People are engaging with pitch/fundraising content, completing an 8-question assessment, and giving their email

Evidence of demand is NOT evidence of willingness to pay $149 for a course. Zero purchase intent data exists yet.

### Four Viable Paths (Ranked)

**Path C (Recommended): Paid ads as list-building for the broader business.**
Accept that paid acquisition at $39-72 CPL is a cost center. The email list is the asset. Monetize through: Eddy course ($149), coaching/War Room ($2K-$5K), community membership, future products. If even 5% of course buyers convert to coaching at $3,500, effective customer LTV = $149 + (0.05 × $3,500) = $324. The list also feeds newsletter growth, social proof, and organic distribution.

This requires evaluating the campaign on list cost and list LTV, not course ROAS.

**Path A: Price up to $497+.**
A $497 course with a $39 CPL needs only 8% lead-to-purchase to break even. That's aggressive but within the range of well-executed launches to segmented lists. This means a different product — more depth, possibly live components, coaching access, or community. Not the lightweight async Eddy currently scoped.

**Path B: Self-liquidating offer funnel.**
Restructure: Quiz → paid action plan ($27-$47) → upsell to full Eddy ($149-$199). The front end covers ad costs. Requires building a checkout flow, an order bump, and a condensed front-end product. More engineering work, but the proven model for sub-$200 courses with paid traffic.

**Path D: Organic only.**
Sell Eddy to your existing audience (newsletter, LinkedIn, podcast). At 3-5% conversion on an organic list of 720/year = 22-36 sales × $149 = $3,300-$5,400/year. Low revenue, zero acquisition cost, pure margin. The quiz becomes an organic conversion tool, not a paid traffic destination.

### What NOT To Do

- Do not evaluate this campaign's success based on whether Eddy's ROAS is positive from paid Meta traffic at $99-$199. It won't be.
- Do not pause the campaign based on CPL alone — if you're choosing Path C, CPL is a list-building cost, not a product acquisition cost.
- Do not assume quiz engagement = willingness to pay. You need actual purchase data before committing to recording the full course.

## Pressure Test

### Strongest Counter-Case

The analysis assumes Eddy is a standalone product in a single-channel funnel. But HC/TNB is building a multi-product ecosystem (course, coaching, community, content). If the $149 course is a gateway to $3,500 coaching, the LTV math changes dramatically. Also: 7 days of data with 15 leads is too thin to declare structural impossibility. CPL could drop 30-40% with optimization (Lead event switch, CAPI, creative iteration, audience learning). At $25 CPL with 5% conversion at $199, CPA = $500 — still underwater on the course alone, but defensible as a list-building cost if downstream LTV is real.

Additionally, founders as a demographic may convert at higher rates than the 1-5% benchmark. They have higher purchasing power, are more action-oriented, and are solving an urgent problem (fundraising). No specific data exists for this niche, but the persona characteristics suggest the low end of benchmarks may not apply.

### Assumptions This Depends On

1. That 1-5% email-to-purchase benchmarks hold for founder audiences (might be higher — founders have urgency and budget)
2. That CPL can't be driven below $25 with further optimization (plausible floor, but unproven)
3. That Eddy remains a standalone async course at $99-$199 (if it evolves into a higher-ticket or bundled offer, the math changes)
4. That the quiz is testing demand for the COURSE, not just demand for free pitch feedback (these are different things)

### Risk Mitigation

- Before committing to recording the full course, run a pre-sale or waitlist test to the existing 15+ leads. Actual purchase intent data > funnel math projections.
- If choosing Path C, define upfront what "acceptable list-building cost" means (e.g., $50/lead for founders who match ICP) so you have a kill switch.
- Track downstream engagement metrics (email open rates, action plan engagement, reply rates) as leading indicators of eventual purchase intent.

## Final Conclusion (Updated April 11, 2026)

### Step-by-Step Funnel Model (Corrected)

The original analysis above was extended into a full step-by-step unit economics model, correcting for several errors in the initial approach: starting from Landing Page View (not impressions) since Meta charges CPM incidentally but LPV is the first meaningful funnel event; using aggregate lead-to-purchase lifecycle conversion (1–5% industry benchmark over 3–6 months of nurture) instead of modeling single-email micro-steps; and clearly separating actual Week 1 data from $10K projection modeling.

**The corrected model at $10K spend (realistic scenario):**
- 2,217 LPVs at $4.51/LPV (actual) → 399 quiz completes (18%) → 140 leads (35%) → CPL of $71
- Lead-to-purchase lifecycle conversion at 2% → 2.8 sales → $322 gross → ~$276 net after refunds/fees
- **12-month full lifecycle (drip + newsletter + retargeting + long tail): $276–$552 returned on $10,000 spent. A 94–97% loss with every touchpoint working.**
- To break even: 52% of leads must purchase (realistic) or 29% (optimistic). Industry best-in-class: 5–10%.

### What About Lookalike Audiences / Retargeting / Other Channels?

These levers primarily improve top-of-funnel metrics (CPL, lead volume). They don't change what happens after someone is on the list. Even if lookalikes cut CPL in half ($35), at 2% lifecycle conversion that yields 5.7 sales × $115 net = $655 on $10K spend — still a 93% loss. The structural problem is lead-to-purchase conversion vs. price point, not traffic quality.

### Brand Coherence Problem

Eddy is a pre-recorded pitching course built under HC positioning: expert-led, top-down instruction on a narrow skill. The New Builder is built on the opposite premise: peer-driven, co-learning, AI-native, broad scope. A founder who buys a $149 pitching course has no natural next step into the War Room, podcast, or TNB community. There is no upsell path. Every dollar and hour spent on Eddy is diverted from the new direction of the company.

### Recommendation

Eddy unit economics evaluated and deemed that profitability is highly unrealistic. Finish the Week 2 checkpoint on April 14 to close the data loop. Then shut down paid ads. Keep the quiz live as a free organic tool. Do not record the course. Redirect hours and budget into TNB priorities.
